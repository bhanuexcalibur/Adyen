01 / Messaging Audit

The platform story is real.
The messaging doesn't always show it.

Adyen's core value is platform consolidation: one contract, one data layer, one reconciliation workflow. But current external messaging often leads with feature-level benefits instead of the structural advantage that actually closes enterprise deals.

Current messaging

The homepage leads with logos of known enterprise clients and a tagline centered on being a "financial technology platform." The value props follow a predictable three-column grid: online payments, in-store, platforms. Each column is self-contained, which fragments what is actually a unified story.

What a CFO sees: Another payments vendor with a lot of names they already recognize. Nothing yet that explains why switching makes commercial sense.

Stronger angle

Lead with the cost of fragmentation, not the breadth of the platform. Enterprise buyers running multiple PSPs know the reconciliation pain, the data gaps, the fraud logic that doesn't carry across channels. That frustration is the hook.

What changes: The primary headline argues for consolidation as a business decision, not a technology purchase. The logos become proof, not decoration.

Core gap: "Financial technology platform" as a label is accurate but not differentiating. Stripe, Checkout.com, and Worldpay all use similar language. The actual differentiator, a single stack with direct scheme connections that spans acquiring, data, and embedded finance under one contract, rarely makes it to the first screen.
Current messaging

Unified Commerce pages describe the feature set well: online + in-store + app, same shopper profile, consistent checkout. The product is genuinely differentiated. The messaging uses the category correctly.

The problem: It's written for someone who already decided they want unified commerce. It doesn't create urgency for the enterprise buyer who is still running separate online and retail contracts and thinks they're managing fine.

Stronger angle

The Unified Commerce story needs a "before" state. Quantify what fragmented data actually costs: the loyalty program that doesn't know about in-store purchases, the fraud model that's flying blind on half the volume, the auth rate that drops because the risk engine has a partial picture.

What changes: Unified Commerce volumes grew 35% YoY in H2 2024. That metric should anchor the commercial case alongside the feature explanation.

Opportunity: The Dr. Martens and H&M case studies exist but aren't aggressive enough about the commercial outcome. A before/after format showing authorization rate lift, reconciliation time saved, and chargeback reduction would make the ROI case much harder to dismiss in a buying committee.
Current messaging

Adyen for Platforms is clearly the fastest-growing segment, with platform volume up 51% in 2024 and embedded financial products issuing volumes growing nearly 3x. The product page is solid on feature coverage: split payments, sub-merchant onboarding, issuing.

The gap: The business model angle for the platform operator is underplayed. Platforms don't just want payments. They want a new revenue stream and tighter user retention. That's a different conversation.

Stronger angle

Position AfP as a monetization decision, not a payments integration. The operator's question is: "what does embedding financial products do for my user retention and ARPU?" that's where the positioning needs to go, especially for SaaS platforms like Toast, Lightspeed, and Zenoti where financial services are becoming a primary margin driver.

What changes: Case study framing shifts from "how we integrated" to "what it added to the business model." Revenue figures for the operator, not just Adyen.

Positioning note: The competitor framing matters here. Stripe Connect is the main comparable. Adyen's edge is the direct scheme access and the ability to support the full embedded finance stack without a second vendor for issuing. That's worth making explicit rather than leaving implicit in the feature list.
02 / GTM Brief

Unified Commerce: enterprise
expansion motion

Adyen's 2025 strategy leans into platform monetization and North American expansion. This brief outlines how a PMM would think about the Unified Commerce GTM motion for mid-market to enterprise retail, where the consolidation argument is sharpest.

Market context

Omnichannel volumes are recovering post-pandemic, but the actual bottleneck for large retailers is data fragmentation, not payment acceptance. Most enterprise brands run separate PSPs for online and in-store, which means their fraud model, loyalty program, and analytics team each have an incomplete picture. Adyen's Unified Commerce directly addresses this.

Competitive landscape

Stripe is strong in online and developer adoption but limited in native POS scale. Worldpay/FIS dominates legacy enterprise but carries integration debt. Adyen's gap: the only credible platform with direct scheme connections, an in-house risk engine, and native in-store hardware that runs on the same data layer as the online stack.

ICP for this motion

Multi-channel retailers with 500M+ in annual revenue, currently running separate online and in-store contracts. Operating in 5+ markets where payment method fragmentation adds compliance and reconciliation overhead. Hospitality, fashion, and QSR verticals show the strongest fit given Adyen's existing customer base.

Buyer committee

Economic buyer: CFO or VP Finance (TCO argument, vendor consolidation). Technical champion: VP Engineering or Head of Payments (integration complexity, uptime SLA). Commercial sponsor: VP Commerce or CRO (authorization lift, conversion rate). Each needs a different message, same platform story.

90-day launch motion
Days 1-30 Insight sprint: Run 8-10 discovery calls with mid-market retail prospects and 3 existing Unified Commerce customers. Map real friction points before building messaging. Identify the language buyers actually use when describing their payment stack problems, not the language the product team uses.
Days 31-60 Messaging validation: Develop two positioning hypotheses (consolidation-led vs. data advantage-led). Test with commercial team in 3 regional markets. Collect win/loss data on 15 recent enterprise deals to identify where messaging lost the room.
Days 61-90 Enablement build: Ship a localized GTM playbook per vertical (retail, hospitality, QSR). One deal calculator showing consolidation cost savings. Two new case studies in the before/after commercial outcome format. Sales battlecard against Stripe and Worldpay at the enterprise tier.

03 / Buyer Map

Three buyers. One platform.
Three different conversations.

Enterprise payments deals rarely have a single decision-maker. The same Adyen pitch needs to land differently depending on who's in the room. Here's how the message shifts without the product story changing.

📈
CFO / VP Finance
Economic buyer, controls vendor consolidation
⚙
VP Engineering
Technical champion, cares about integration risk
📱
CRO / VP Commerce
Commercial sponsor, owns conversion and revenue
Economic buyer
The CFO doesn't buy payment features. They buy fewer vendors on the P&L and better working capital visibility.
What they care about

Vendor count, reconciliation cost, FX exposure, settlement timing, and total cost of payments as a percentage of revenue. They want to know: what do I stop paying for, and where does the number go down?

Where Adyen wins

Single contract covering all channels eliminates multi-vendor reconciliation overhead. Direct scheme connections on Mastercard and Visa mean lower interchange routing costs. Unified settlement across 150+ currencies reduces FX complexity significantly.

Suggested opening line
"Most enterprise payment stacks grew organically, one vendor per channel, one contract per market. We see what that costs in reconciliation hours and missed authorization revenue. There's a cleaner way to run this."
Technical champion
The engineering lead wants to know what breaks during migration and who owns the problem if it does.
What they care about

API stability, SLA guarantees, documentation quality, SDK coverage, and the rollback plan. They've been burned before. The question is always: how many engineers does this take to maintain, and who do I call at 2am?

Where Adyen wins

Single API for all channels, all payment methods, all markets. In-house risk engine with configurable rules that don't require professional services to modify. Dedicated technical account manager at enterprise tier. The integration surface area is actually smaller than managing five vendor relationships.

Suggested opening line
"Running five payment vendors means five integration surfaces, five risk models with no shared context, and five upgrade cycles you didn't ask for. One API that handles all of it isn't a feature, it's a maintenance cost argument."
Commercial sponsor
The revenue owner cares about authorization rates, checkout conversion, and not leaving revenue on the table at the payment step.
What they care about

Authorization rate improvement, decline recovery, local payment method coverage in growth markets, and the shopper experience at checkout. Every declined transaction is a lost sale. Every failed retry is friction they hear about from the growth team.

Where Adyen wins

Adyen Uplift (launched January 2025) showed up to 6% improvement in payment conversion in 2024 pilots. Intelligent Payment Routing for US Debit, introduced in 2024, cut costs 26% for pilot customers. The RevenueAccelerate product suite turns authorization data into a retention tool, not just a compliance metric.

Suggested opening line
"Authorization rate is a revenue metric, not a payments metric. A 1% improvement at 500M in volume is 5M that was already in your funnel. That's the conversation Adyen starts differently."

Product launch copy · Adyen Uplift (January 2025) Launch
The payment optimization tool that turns decline data into revenue recovery.
Adyen Uplift applies machine learning across your full transaction history to increase payment conversion. In a 2024 pilot, merchants saw up to 6% improvement in authorization rates, without changing their checkout flow or their shopper experience.

It works because Adyen processes both sides of the transaction. The risk model that flags the decline is the same model that learns from it. No third-party optimization layer. No data handoffs. Just a better decision, faster.
Why this works: Leads with the outcome, not the feature name. Explains the architectural advantage (same-stack processing) in plain language. The "without changing" line directly addresses the VP Engineering's integration concern in the middle of a commercial message.
Thought leadership · LinkedIn long-form Demand gen
Why payment data is the loyalty program asset most brands don't know they have.
A shopper buys online on Monday. Returns an item in-store on Thursday. Uses a different card for both transactions.

Most payment stacks see two separate events. No shared identity, no connected profile, no behavioral signal passed to the loyalty engine or the fraud model.

The brands doing this well, the ones where checkout feels like it knows the customer, are running a unified data layer underneath. Not three vendors with partial views.

Payment consolidation stopped being an IT decision somewhere around 2022. It's a data strategy decision now.
Why this works: Opens with a scenario, not a claim. The emotional hook is recognition: every CRO has seen the loyalty program gap. The conclusion lands without needing to say "Adyen" until much later in the content funnel.
Sales battlecard snippet · vs. Stripe Connect Enablement
When they say: "We're already using Stripe."
Stripe is a strong default for developer-first online payments. If the buyer's world is web-only, fast iteration, and domestic volume, that conversation can end there.

The question to ask: "What does your in-store payment stack look like, and who owns the reconciliation between the two?"

If the answer is a second vendor, or "finance handles it manually," that's the gap. Adyen processes both channels on the same platform, same risk engine, same settlement. No bridge integrations. No reconciliation gap. For enterprise retail doing over 500M annually, the consolidation math usually favors Adyen by a measurable margin on total cost of payments.
Why this works: Doesn't dismiss Stripe. Acknowledges where they're genuinely strong. Then pivots to the specific scenario where Adyen wins clearly: multi-channel enterprise with reconciliation complexity. Gives the sales rep a specific question to use rather than a feature list to read from.
05 / Launch Playbook

How a product launch actually runs
at an enterprise scale.

Using Adyen Uplift as the reference case: an AI-powered payment optimization product launched in January 2025. Here's the playbook structure for a PMM overseeing a global launch across 20+ markets.

Adyen Uplift: global launch playbook

January 2025 launch · Multi-market · Enterprise tier focus

Phase 01
Positioning foundation
Weeks 1-4
Messaging Research Stakeholder alignment
Align on what Uplift is and isn't selling: it's not a feature, it's an authorization rate improvement, measured in basis points and translated to revenue. Run positioning sessions with product, commercial, and risk teams. Identify the one-line proof point from the 2024 pilot (up to 6% conversion improvement) and build backwards from it. Determine which buyer tier gets which message: existing merchant upsell vs. new enterprise prospect.
Phase 02
Market segmentation
Weeks 3-6
Localization brief Vertical mapping Regional commercial teams
Authorization rate sensitivity varies by market: higher in the US where debit routing is complex, different in markets with local payment method dominance. Brief regional PMMs on where the Uplift story lands hardest by vertical. For retail: conversion rate framing. For subscriptions: involuntary churn reduction framing. For travel: approval rate at high-value ticket transactions. Each market gets a localized proof point, not a translated version of the global copy.
Phase 03
Enablement build
Weeks 5-8
Sales deck Deal calculator Objection handling One-pager
Ship a deal calculator that takes a merchant's current processed volume and current authorization rate and outputs the revenue opportunity at various improvement scenarios. This becomes the first slide in every commercial conversation. Separate the "what is Uplift" deck (for new prospects) from the "here's your Uplift opportunity" deck (for account expansion conversations). Build the objection doc around the three most common blockers from the pilot feedback: data privacy concerns, integration timeline, and "we're satisfied with our current rate."
Phase 04
Launch execution
Weeks 8-12
Press and analyst Merchant comms Content wave Event activation
Coordinate the external launch with a case study from a named pilot merchant (with permission) and a press release anchoring on the 6% pilot outcome. Activate at Money20/20 or Shoptalk if timing allows, where the enterprise buyer is already in the room. Run the internal launch as a commercial team kickoff: 45-minute live session showing the deal calculator in action, covering the objection playbook, and walking through the first 30-day pipeline target. Measure adoption within 30 days of ship.
Phase 05
Measurement
Weeks 10 onwards
Pipeline tracking Win/loss review Adoption rate
Track: pipeline generated from Uplift-led conversations in the first 90 days. Win rate on deals where the deal calculator was used vs. not used. Enablement asset adoption rate across the commercial team. Review win/loss notes at 60 days to identify where the message is falling short and iterate the objection handling. Report back to product with the three most common questions buyers ask that the current materials don't answer.
